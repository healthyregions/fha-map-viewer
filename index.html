Block Data Maps were originally drafted by the Civil Works Administration and the Department of Commerce as a part of the Real Property Inventory (RPI) which began in November 1933. On June 27, 1934, the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) was established by the National Housing Act.

Soon thereafter, the FHA recognized the value of the Block Data Maps and began working on the Real Property Inventory which produced the original Block Data Maps. After refining and modifying the RPI, it was rebranded the Real Property Survey.

According to the June 1937 article "A New Map Aid for Mortgage Lenders" published in the FHA's trade journal Insured Mortgage Portfolio, the FHA saw Block Data Maps as vitally important tools for underwriting or guaranteeing loans.

The article stated that Block Data Maps could help mortgage lenders determine "whether there are inharmonious racial groups in the vicinity" (p. 9) and that appraisers could consult the map to "refresh his memory as to the danger points in the neighborhood" (p. 9).

Unlike the Federal Home Loan Bank Board's technically named Residential Security Maps (commonly called HOLC maps), the FHA Block Data maps used the color green to denote low-rent paying blocks, while the color red was used for higher rent paying blocks. The highest rent paying blocks were blue or purple.

While the color scheme differed, the spatial logic of the maps was the same. The FHA deployed Block Data Maps as a means to determine where they would underwrite or not underwrite loans based largely on ability to pay and the proximity of "inharmonious racial groups."

Two independent teams began recovering these maps and scanning them at the National Archives II in August 2022. The team at the Center for Urban Health Equity (CUHE) located at Morgan State University was led by Dr. Lawrence Brown and research associate Jehan Reaves. Dr. Brown has published on the FHA maps, while Jehan Reaves has published on the spread of Federal Home Loan Bank Board maps.

The second independent team consisted of Wenfei Xu, Jacob Faber, Thomas Storrs, and Katherine Thomas. We encourage visitors to follow their work to learn more about the research they are conducting with the FHA Block Data Maps.

CUHE is fortunate to have the friendship and partnership of the Healthy Regions and Policy Lab (HeROP) at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign led by Dr. Marynia Kolak. Through the CUHE-HeROP partnership, we were introduced to Adam Cox who has led our georeferencing efforts. Adam Cox created and designed the FHA Block Data Map viewer, which is a joint CUHE-HeROP project.

Long believed to be lost, the FHA Block Data Maps reveal over 150 maps that the Federal Housing Administration (FHA) prepared during the 1930s. These maps provide insight into the work of the New Deal Mapping State. Use the viewer to compare the maps with the maps of cities in the 2020s.
